Title:First, Second and Third Massive Stars in Open Clusters
 
Edited V1

Report on ApJ/341471/ART
"First, Second and Third Massive Stars in Open Clusters"
by A. Mints

The paper describes an interesting method to determine the total mass
and number of stars of a star cluster by using only the most-massive
stars. This is done for three different methods of sampling stars from
the IMF as well as for different values which describe the distribution
of n-th most-massive star, the mean, median and mode. Easy to apply
formulae to obtain the total cluster mass and number of stars by only
using the 1st, 2nd or 3rd massive star in the cluster are derived for
the different cases and their errors are evaluated by comparing with
numerical Monte Carlo experiments.

However, I cannot recommend publication of the work before significant
changes, improvements or clarifications are made.


1) In the introduction, the dynamical mass estimation of star clusters
through the virial theorem is briefly mentioned. I think it would help
the cause of the paper to point out that besides the larger amount of
observational data required to do so, such dynamical masses also suffer
from the usually unknown binary properties of the cluster members
(Kouwenhoven & de Grijs, 2009, Ap&SS, 324, 171).
The introduction should also mentioned the "photometric" approach to
determine cluster masses - transformation of the magnitudes and colors
of suspected member stars via evolutionary models and then integration
of the derived mass function with or without extrapolation beyond the
observational lower limit.


2) Again only briefly mentioned in the introduction is the proposed
relation between the cluster mass and the most-massive star in a
cluster by Weidner & Kroupa (2006). Here it should be pointed out that
Maschberger & Clarke (2008) actually argue against such a relation and
that while Weidner, Kroupa & Bonnell (2010, MNRAS, 401, 275)
re-investigate this issue, the existence of a physical correlation
rather than a statistical one is not beyond doubt.
As the author propagates the use of the median or the mode of the
distribution of the n-th most-massive star rather than the average, it
might be of use to note that Weidner, Kroupa & Bonnell (2010) also
discuss this topic but for the most-massive star alone.


3) Still in the introduction, the author tells us that he is going to
neglect stellar binarity and evolution. Here my main concern is a
third important factor: dynamical ejections of massive stars (see for
example Pflamm-Altenburg & Kroupa, 2006, MNRAS, 373, 295 and
Schillbach & Roeser, 2008, A&A, 489, 105). The ejection of the
most-massive star might be unlikely because in triple interactions
usually the least-massive star is ejected, but as O stars are often in
binaries with other O stars (e.g. Sana et al. 2009, MNRAS, 400, 1479
and Sana et al. 2008, MNRAS, 386, 447), which tend to group in the
core of young clusters (e.g. Kerber & Santiago, 2006, A&A, 452, 155),
the dynamical loss of the 2nd or 3rd massive star might be much more
common.
How much would such an event change the results of this analysis?
Is there any way to estimate the error if the 2nd or 3rd massive stars is
ejected?
This might also give an error estimate for unresolved binaries. If,
e.g., the 2nd and 3rd massive star are in a close orbit, they might
"look" like the most-massive star and henceforth, the actually
most-massive one is used as the 2nd massive one.


4) Generally, I also miss a broader discussion of the relevant
literature in the introduction. For example, Elmegreen (2000, ApJ, 539,
342) shows how the cluster mass should scale with the mass of the
most-massive star (M_cl ~ 3 10^3 (m_max/100)^1.35) when no limit for
the IMF is assumed. Such a result should be compared with authors new
findings.


5) The definition of the so-called "sorted sampling" at the beginning
of Section 2 differs somewhat from the sorted sampling in Weidner &
Kroupa (2006) (WK06). WK06 take a number of stars, calculated by
dividing a given cluster mass by the mean of the used IMF, randomly
from the IMF, sorted them by mass and add them up starting with the
lowest mass star. If this does not reach the given cluster mass, the
difference is again divided by the mean from the IMF and this
additional number of stars is randomly taken from the IMF. Then all
stars are again sorted by mass and summed up. If necessary, this is
repeated until the cluster mass is reached.
It is not clear to me, if the author proceeded in the same way or
not. This should be clarified.


6) In his results Section (4) mass and number estimator functions
based on the average and median and mode values are defined. But Tab.1
only gives the fit formula parameters for the average and median
values but not for the mode. But in Tab.2 and 3 the errors and
dispersion are given for the mode as well. The fit parameters for the
mode should be added to Tab.1.


7) On page 9 (Section 4.1) the errors shown in Tab.2 and 3 are
discussed. Here I do not fully understand the numbers given in the
text when compared to the tables. When using the average estimator
errors up to 40% are given on page 9 but in Tab.2 the largest error I
see is 23.24%. Why?
For the median estimator, it is quoted that the error is always less than 3%
which is true, but I find that they are always less than 2% in Tab.2.
The mode estimator values of Tab.2 are not discussed at all but, at
least according to Tab.2, it quite often has larger errors than the
average estimator.
In contrast to this in Tab.3, the mode estimator gives always by far the lowest
dispersion, while the average estimator can result in extremely large
dispersions.
Therefore, Tables 2 and 3 need to be discussed in more detail.


8) Section 4.4 discusses the estimator reliability. It is noted that
the calculated differences for different sampling methods and 1st, 2nd
and 3rd massive star, as shown in Tab.4, remain large but this result
is not discussed at all. Why?
Especially notable for me was the generally huge difference (50 to
70%) when using the mode estimator for the 2nd and 3rd massive
star. This needs to be discussed in that section.
The last two paragraphs of Section 4.4 address a second reliability
check - applying the estimators to the "wrong" dataset. Unfortunately,
the results of this check are only summarized in the paper but not
shown in detail. Maybe it is possible to add them as an additional
column in Tab.4.


9) The conclusions (Section 5) state that the median or the mode
estimators are better than the average estimator. While I agree on the
median estimator when looking at the tables 2 and 4, I do not see why
the mode is supposed to be a "good" one. Only Tab.3 might support this
conclusion. In general the results of the mode estimator are not well
discussed in the paper at all.


10) The English of the paper clearly needs improvement. Sentences like
"Using median values minimizes error for highest fraction of the data,
while for the small fraction errors remain large" are not easy to
understand.
It would also be helpful if the figures would be larger (especially
Fig.1 and 3). It is nearly impossible to differentiate solid,
long-dashed and short-dashed lines in Fig.3.
Additionally, several of the references in the text have a strange
format, for example "Maschberger & Clarke Maschberger & Clarke
(2008)". This needs to be fixed.
